How are inmates screened and treated for mental illness?

By Julia Haslanger & Adriele Parker

Intro

Lede: In New York, nearly 40 percent of the city’s jail inmates suffer from a mental illness. At the city’s primary jail, Rikers Island, 67 percent have indicated a mental health issue and 21 percent are “severely mentally ill.”

Nutgraf: This isn’t a problem for just a few people. Almost two-thirds of inmates surveyed in state prisons have at least one mental illness, according to a 2004 survey by the Bureau of Justice Statistics. Prisons and jails don’t have a uniform way of screening for or treating mental illnesses. Those inconsistencies are especially problematic as inmates are transferred from one prison to another during their sentences. And when inmates leave the correctional system, they can often struggle to find treatment for their illnesses.

And New York is aware it has a problem on its hands when inmates rejoin the world without mental health support. A new law allows for more access to treatment for recently released prisoners who received mental health treatments during their last three years, the AP reports.
