How are inmates screened and treated for mental illness?

By Julia Haslanger & Adriele Parker

Intro

Lede: You might think that getting out of prison would be the best feeling in the world, but for inmates with mental illnesses, being released can come with the challenge of losing access to treatment. In New York, nearly 40 percent of the city’s jail inmates suffer from a mental illness. At the city’s primary jail, Rikers Island, 67 percent have indicated a mental health issue and 21 percent are “severely mentally ill.”

And New York is aware it has a problem on its hands when inmates rejoin the world without mental health support. A new law allows for more access to treatment for recently released prisoners who received mental health treatments during their last three years, the AP reports.

Nutgraf: This isn’t a problem for just a few people. Almost two-thirds of inmates surveyed in state prisons have at least one mental illness, according to a 2004 survey by the Bureau of Justice Statistics. Prisons don’t have a uniform way of screening for or treating mental illnesses. Those inconsistencies are especially problematic as inmates are transferred from one prison to another during their sentences.

Inmates are also less likely (maybe? source?) to receive therapy for the same illnesses as those outside the system, and more likely to be prescribed drugs — drugs that won’t necessarily be re-supplied/available after the inmate’s release.
